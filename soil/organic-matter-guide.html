Organic Matter Management

Learn how to maintain and improve soil organic matter for better soil health

What is Soil Organic Matter?

Soil organic matter (SOM) consists of plant and animal residues at various stages of decomposition, cells and tissues of soil organisms, and substances synthesized by soil microbes. It's a critical component of healthy, productive soil and typically makes up just 2-10% of the soil's mass but has a disproportionately large influence on soil properties.

Why Organic Matter is Important

Organic matter in soil provides numerous benefits that are essential for sustainable agriculture:

Physical Benefits

  • Improved Soil Structure: Creates soil aggregates, reducing compaction
  • Enhanced Water Infiltration: Allows water to move more easily through soil
  • Increased Water Holding Capacity: Helps soil retain moisture during dry periods
  • Reduced Erosion Risk: Binds soil particles together, preventing erosion
  • Better Aeration: Creates pore spaces for air movement, essential for root health

Chemical Benefits

  • Nutrient Storage: Holds and gradually releases nutrients
  • Enhanced CEC: Increases Cation Exchange Capacity, improving nutrient availability
  • pH Buffering: Helps stabilize soil pH
  • Reduced Leaching: Minimizes nutrient loss through water movement

Biological Benefits

  • Microbial Habitat: Provides food and shelter for beneficial soil organisms
  • Enhanced Biodiversity: Supports a wider range of soil life
  • Disease Suppression: Some soil microbes help control plant pathogens

Science Fact

Just a 1% increase in soil organic matter can help soil hold approximately 20,000 more gallons of water per acre.

Measuring Soil Organic Matter

Before implementing strategies to increase organic matter, it's important to know your baseline levels. Several methods exist to measure SOM:

  • Loss on Ignition: Sample is weighed, burned at high temperature, and reweighed to determine organic content
  • Walkley-Black Method: Uses chemical oxidation to measure organic carbon
  • Lab Testing Services: Professional soil labs can provide accurate SOM measurements
  • Visual Assessment: Dark-colored soils often (but not always) indicate higher organic matter

Strategies to Increase Soil Organic Matter

1. Add Organic Amendments

Directly adding organic materials is one of the most effective ways to increase SOM:

  • Compost: 1-2 inches applied annually (approximately 3-6 tons per acre)
  • Animal Manure: Well-aged manure provides nutrients and organic matter
  • Biochar: Highly stable carbon that can persist in soil for centuries
  • Mulch: Organic mulches like straw or wood chips that break down over time

2. Implement Cover Crops

Cover crops protect the soil while adding organic matter:

  • Legumes: Clover, vetch, peas (also fix nitrogen)
  • Grasses: Rye, oats, wheat (produce significant biomass)
  • Brassicas: Radish, turnip (help break up compacted soil)
  • Mixtures: Combinations often provide multiple benefits

Pro Tip

For maximum organic matter contribution, terminate cover crops at flowering stage when biomass is highest but before they set seed.

3. Practice Crop Rotation

Diversifying crops helps build soil organic matter by:

  • Including high-residue crops like corn and small grains
  • Incorporating deep-rooted crops that add organic matter below the surface
  • Alternating between different crop families to improve overall soil health

4. Minimize Tillage

Excessive tillage accelerates organic matter decomposition. Consider:

  • No-till systems: Planting directly into previous crop residues
  • Reduced tillage: Limiting depth and frequency of soil disturbance
  • Strip tillage: Only disturbing the planting row

5. Manage Crop Residues

Crop residues are a valuable source of organic matter:

  • Leave crop residues in the field rather than removing them
  • Chop or shred residues to speed decomposition
  • Consider adding nitrogen to help break down high-carbon residues like corn stalks

Common Challenges and Solutions

Challenge: Slow Results

Solution: Building organic matter takes timeâ€”often 3-5 years of consistent practices before significant changes are measurable. Start with realistic expectations and monitor trends rather than expecting immediate results.

Challenge: Sandy Soils

Solution: Sandy soils warm quickly and have high oxygen levels, which accelerate organic matter decomposition. More frequent, smaller additions of organic matter often work better than infrequent large applications.

Challenge: Heavy Clay Soils

Solution: Clay soils may need initial physical improvements. Consider using deep-rooted cover crops like daikon radish alongside organic matter additions.

Challenge: Arid Conditions

Solution: In dry climates, focus on water-efficient practices like mulching and drought-tolerant cover crops. Consider reduced rates of irrigation to support decomposer organisms.

Monitoring Progress

Tracking changes in soil organic matter helps you adjust your management practices:

  • Test soil every 2-3 years at the same time of year
  • Keep detailed records of all organic inputs
  • Monitor other soil health indicators like infiltration rate and earthworm counts
  • Take dated photographs of soil profiles to visually track changes

Conclusion

Building and maintaining soil organic matter is a long-term investment in your soil's health and productivity. While it requires consistent effort and patience, the rewards include reduced input costs, greater resilience to weather extremes, and sustained or improved yields over time. Remember that even small increases in soil organic matter can have significant benefits for your farming operation.
